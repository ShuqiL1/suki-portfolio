Main Case Study

Designing a Guided Vehicle Photo Capture Flow

Vehicle photo capture is a critical part of the automotive listing and review process, but users can easily submit the wrong photo type, miss required shots, or provide low-quality images when the flow is unclear.


I worked on Photo Flow, a mobile-first guided capture experience that helps dealership users take the right vehicle photos quickly and consistently. My role focused on structuring the PRD, defining the core user flow, clarifying V1 and V2 scope, and translating business requirements into clear UX behaviours for design and development handoff.

Photo Flow Context

Vehicle photos are an important part of the automotive listing and review process. They help the business assess vehicle condition, ensure photo coverage, and maintain consistent listing quality. However, photo capture can easily become inconsistent when users are in a hurry or unsure what angle, object, or quality standard is expected. The business needed a flow that could guide users without slowing them down.

The flow starts from the vehicle record, guides users through camera-based capture, and allows them to review the completed media set before submission.

Problem Statement

The existing vehicle photo capture process created several operational risks. Users could take the wrong photo type, submit blurry or dark photos, miss mandatory shots, or require follow-up corrections after submission. These issues increased rework for the team and made it difficult to assess photo quality consistently at scale.

Design Challenge

The challenge was to balance speed and compliance: users needed a fast capture flow, while the business needed complete, high-quality, and reviewable photo evidence.

Project Overview

EDI is an inclusive design project focused on understanding how digital experiences can better support users with diverse needs, backgrounds, and access conditions. The project explored how design can reduce barriers, improve clarity, and create a more respectful and inclusive user experience.

My role involved researching user needs, identifying accessibility and inclusion barriers, translating insights into design principles, and developing design responses that support clearer and more equitable access.

Design Context

Digital products are often designed around an assumed "average user", but real users have different abilities, language backgrounds, confidence levels, cultural expectations, and access conditions. When these differences are not considered, the experience can become confusing, exclusionary, or difficult to use.

This project focused on designing with inclusion in mind, ensuring that the experience is not only functional, but also accessible, understandable, and respectful for a wider range of users.

Design Challenge

How might we design a digital experience that reduces access barriers and supports users with diverse needs in a clearer, more inclusive way?

01 / Reduce access barriers

The experience should be easier to understand and use for people with different levels of confidence, ability, and digital familiarity.

02 / Improve clarity

Information should be structured in a way that is easy to scan, understand, and act on.

03 / Support respectful representation

The design should avoid assumptions and create a more inclusive experience for different user groups.

My Role

As a UX Designer and Researcher, I focused on understanding user barriers and translating inclusive design principles into practical design decisions. My role involved research synthesis, problem framing, design principle development, concept exploration, and interface refinement.

Research Focus

Understanding Barriers Before Designing Solutions

Before moving into design, I focused on identifying what kinds of barriers users might experience when interacting with the service or digital interface. These barriers were not only functional, but also emotional, cognitive, social, and accessibility-related.

Information barriers

Users may struggle when information is too complex, hidden, or written in unclear language.

Accessibility barriers

Users may experience difficulty due to low contrast, small text, unclear navigation, or limited interaction options.

Confidence barriers

Some users may hesitate when they are unsure what action to take or whether they are making the right choice.

Representation barriers

Users may feel excluded when the experience assumes one default background, ability, or behaviour pattern.

Key Insights

INSIGHT 01

Clarity is part of inclusion

Inclusive design is not only about adding accessibility features. Clear language, visible structure, and predictable navigation can reduce cognitive load and help more users feel confident.

INSIGHT 02

Users need flexibility, not a one-size-fits-all path

Different users may need different levels of guidance, information detail, or interaction support. The experience should allow users to move through the journey in a way that suits their needs.

INSIGHT 03

Representation affects trust

The tone, imagery, labels, and content structure can influence whether users feel recognised, respected, and included.

INSIGHT 04

Accessibility should be embedded, not added at the end

Accessibility considerations such as contrast, readable text, clear hierarchy, and simple interactions should shape the design from the beginning.

Inclusive Design Principles

PRINCIPLE 01

Clear

Use plain language, visible structure, and clear calls to action to reduce confusion.

PRINCIPLE 02

Flexible

Support different user behaviours, confidence levels, and access needs.

PRINCIPLE 03

Respectful

Avoid assumptions and use inclusive language and representation.

PRINCIPLE 04

Accessible

Prioritise readable typography, sufficient contrast, simple navigation, and screen-friendly layouts.

PRINCIPLE 05

Supportive

Provide guidance at moments where users may hesitate or need reassurance.

Key Design Decisions

Design Decision 1: Make sustainable action feel small and achievable

Users may feel sustainability is difficult when it requires major changes. I designed the experience around small daily actions, making kitchen waste reuse feel manageable and easy to repeat.

B
Simple input reduces effort

D

Design Decision 2: Visualise progress to maintain motivation

Sustainability impact can feel invisible. ReSoil makes progress visible through soil growth, habit streaks, and contribution summaries, helping users understand the value of their repeated actions.

Soil progress shows visible transformation
B
Streaks encourage consistency

Impact summary connects action to outcome
D
Milestones reward continued participation

Design Decision 3: Use positive reinforcement instead of guilt

Rather than relying on negative messaging about waste, the app uses encouraging feedback, rewards, and growth-oriented visuals to make sustainable behaviour feel satisfying.

Design Decision 4: Connect digital tracking with real-world action

The app is designed to support a physical household habit. Digital reminders and progress tracking are connected to the real-world process of collecting kitchen waste and cultivating soil.

Final Design

The final prototype presents ReSoil as a friendly and motivating sustainability companion, helping users understand what to reuse, track their progress, and build a more sustainable household routine.

Designathon Process

Designing Under a 24-hour Constraint

Because the project was created during a 24-hour designathon, we had to make fast product decisions and focus on the clearest version of the concept. Instead of exploring every possible sustainability feature, we prioritised a simple behaviour loop that could be explained, prototyped, and pitched within the limited timeframe.

Reflection

This project helped me understand how important focus is in rapid product design. Under a 24-hour constraint, the strongest idea is not always the one with the most features, but the one with the clearest behaviour loop and story.

I also learned that sustainability design should not rely only on awareness or guilt. To create lasting behaviour change, the experience needs to make the action easy, visible, and rewarding.

ReSoil strengthened my ability to turn a broad social issue into a focused product concept, collaborate quickly with a team, and communicate design value through a clear pitch narrative.
